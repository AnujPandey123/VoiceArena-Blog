What 44,387 Human Votes Reveal About TTS Failure Across Six Languages

Leaderboards tell you who won. Human feedback tells you what broke. Here is what evaluators actually complained about , and why the patterns differ more by language than most model teams expect.

VoiceArena Research · 44,387 votes · English, Hindi, Japanese, Portuguese, Vietnamese, Arabic

Most text-to-speech evaluations end with a ranking. One model scores higher than another, a leaderboard is updated, a benchmark report is published. That ranking is useful. It answers one question: which model won? But for the teams building these systems, the ranking is usually the least interesting part of the result.

The more important question is almost always different: why did people prefer one voice over another? And when they rejected a model, what exactly did they hear?

VoiceArena's evaluation framework gives us an unusual way to answer that. Every vote is based on a direct comparison , two speech outputs from two different models, generated from the same text. Evaluators listen to both and choose the one they prefer. In many cases, they also leave a written comment explaining their decision.

Those comments, combined with structured defect tags, create something that most speech benchmarks don't have: a large, language-stratified collection of human explanations for model failure. We analyzed 44,387 votes spanning English, Hindi, Japanese, Portuguese, Vietnamese, and Arabic. Rather than focusing on who ranked where, we looked at what evaluators repeatedly complained about , and where those complaints clustered.

How VoiceArena Works

VoiceArena uses pairwise evaluation. For every vote, the same sentence is synthesized by two different models. Model identities are hidden. A human evaluator listens to both outputs and picks the preferred one. Defects can be tagged, and , critically , comments can be left explaining the decision.

This structure sidesteps one of the persistent problems in traditional Mean Opinion Score evaluations: calibration drift. Instead of asking whether a voice deserves a 4.2 or a 4.4, evaluators answer a simpler question: which one sounds better? The vote captures the preference. The comment captures the reason.

A ranking tells you where you stand. A comment often tells you what to fix. Those are not the same thing, and conflating them is how teams end up optimizing for the wrong problem.

The specific ranking within these categories matters less than the broader picture: the vast majority of complaints fall into three areas , prosody, pronunciation, and timing. Listeners rarely reject a model because they couldn't understand it. They reject it because it doesn't sound like a person talking.

Prosody: Still the Hardest Problem to Solve

The single most common defect in the dataset , appearing nearly 17,000 times , is unnatural or robotic speech. Despite years of rapid improvement in synthesis quality, listeners continue to notice when speech lacks the natural variation in pitch, emphasis, rhythm, and phrasing that characterizes real human delivery.

The words evaluators reach for in their comments are consistent: robotic, flat, monotone, mechanical, lifeless. These descriptions appear across nearly every language in the dataset. The pattern carries a direct implication for model teams: intelligibility is no longer the primary battlefield. A sentence can be perfectly understandable and still lose a pairwise comparison because it sounds generated rather than spoken.

This matters because many automated evaluation metrics struggle with exactly this distinction. Word Error Rate, UTMOS, and speaker similarity scores can all look healthy while the prosody remains unconvincing. A model can produce every word correctly and still fail to produce convincing speech. Human evaluators consistently catch that gap. Automated metrics often don't.

Pronunciation Failures Are Larger Than Most Benchmarks Suggest

Combined, mild and severe mispronunciation account for more than 26,000 tagged defects , making pronunciation the single largest category by volume if you treat both severity levels together. The complaints span incorrect phonemes, wrong stress patterns, foreign accent leakage into the target language, and language-specific errors that only become visible when you evaluate outside English.

This is where the multilingual structure of VoiceArena's dataset becomes genuinely informative. A model that benchmarks well in English can still carry phonetic weaknesses that only surface in other languages. Those weaknesses are invisible in English-only evaluation , not because they don't exist, but because English evaluation doesn't create the conditions that expose them.

The comments make these failures visible in a way that aggregate scores don't. An evaluator noting that a Hindi model mispronounced a specific consonant cluster, or that an Arabic model dropped a vowel diacritic, gives the model team something they can actually act on.

The Failure Profile Changes Substantially by Language

One of the more striking findings in the dataset is that the distribution of complaint types shifts considerably across languages. There is no single universal failure profile. A model's weakest point depends heavily on the language being evaluated , which means global rankings can obscure as much as they reveal.

English

Dominated by prosody complaints. Evaluators gravitate toward whichever output sounds more conversational, even when both are technically correct. Pacing and rhythm are strong differentiators.

Hindi

A more even split between robotic delivery and pronunciation errors. The mix suggests that gains driven by English preference data don't automatically transfer , Hindi-specific training is meaningful here.

Arabic

The strongest concentration of pronunciation complaints in the dataset. Phonetic accuracy is a more pressing problem than expressiveness. Model teams working on Arabic may find bigger gains there than in prosody work.

Japanese

Small mistakes in rhythm or phoneme placement carry disproportionate perceptual weight. Evaluators were sensitive to both timing and pronunciation errors in ways that closely affected their preference decisions.

Vietnamese

Robotic delivery is the dominant complaint. Natural prosody appears to be a stronger quality signal than basic intelligibility , evaluators care more about how it sounds than whether it's technically accurate.

Portuguese

Similar profile to English. Naturalness and delivery drive most preference decisions. The complaints cluster around expression and rhythm rather than phoneme-level errors.

The practical consequence is worth stating directly: if your team is building a multilingual model and you're only running evaluations in English, you likely have a distorted picture of where the system actually fails. Language-specific evaluation isn't optional for teams that want to deploy across multiple markets.

What Evaluators Notice That Metrics Often Miss

Standard TTS evaluation tends to center on a familiar set of measurements: Word Error Rate, Mean Opinion Score, UTMOS, speaker similarity. These are genuinely useful. The VoiceArena data suggests they don't capture the full picture of why people reject speech outputs.

The gap is most acute in prosody. Rhythm, emphasis, pacing, and expressiveness are the factors that most frequently drive preference decisions in the dataset , and they remain difficult to measure without human listeners. As synthesis quality continues to improve across the industry, these higher-order characteristics become increasingly important. The difference between first place and fifth place in a competitive field is often no longer intelligibility. It's delivery.

Why Comments Beat Rankings for Model Improvement

Consider two models with identical win rates. One is losing because evaluators find its voice robotic. The other is losing because of pronunciation mistakes. A leaderboard treats both models as equally competitive. The problems underneath are completely different, and the fixes required are completely different too , one team needs to work on prosody modeling, the other on phoneme handling.

Without the comment layer, those distinctions are hard to identify. With it, they become diagnostic. This is the structural advantage of VoiceArena's setup: the comment converts a preference signal into something actionable. A model that loses on prosody repeatedly, across multiple prompts and multiple evaluators, has been handed a fairly specific research direction. A model losing on pronunciation in Arabic but not in English has been told something about the coverage of its training data.

The vote tells you which model won. The comment tells you why the other one lost. For a team trying to improve a system, the second sentence is almost always more useful than the first.

What Model Builders Should Take From This

  • Evaluate by language, not just overall. Global rankings are useful summaries, but they regularly hide language-specific failure patterns. A model that ranks third overall might rank first in English and sixth in Arabic. Those are different problems and they require different interventions.
  • Separate prosody, pronunciation, pacing, and audio quality in your tracking. Treating these as a single quality score loses most of the useful signal. Each category points to a different part of the system , prosody modeling, phoneme handling, training data coverage, codec quality. Grouping them together obscures the actual bottleneck.
  • Look for recurring complaints, not just bad votes. A single negative vote tells you little. A pattern of evaluators noting the same problem , "flat delivery on longer sentences," "Arabic stress consistently wrong," "pacing breaks before punctuation" , tells you something systematic. That's where investigation is worth spending time.
  • Don't anchor on win rate alone. Two models with the same win rate may have entirely different improvement paths. The comments provide the context that the score doesn't. A model losing narrowly on prosody is in a different position than one losing narrowly on pronunciation , even if both numbers look the same on a leaderboard.

What This Dataset Tells Us About the State of TTS

Across 44,387 votes and six languages, human evaluators are remarkably consistent about why they reject speech outputs. The complaints cluster tightly: robotic delivery, pronunciation errors, pacing problems, timing mistakes. Audio quality issues exist but rank well below the others.

That consistency is itself a finding. It means the leading failure modes in TTS are not scattered and idiosyncratic , they're predictable and repeatable. And predictable failure modes are fixable ones, provided you have enough signal to see them clearly.

The multilingual dimension adds a layer of complexity that single-language evaluation simply can't surface. English and Arabic don't produce the same complaint patterns. Japanese and Portuguese don't fail in the same places. A model that has genuinely closed the gap on robotic delivery in English may have barely touched the pronunciation problems that matter most in Arabic.

Leaderboards will continue to be useful for orientation , for understanding roughly where a model sits relative to its peers. But as the top of those leaderboards gets more competitive, the margin between systems will increasingly come down to the specific, language-sensitive failure modes that only human evaluation can reliably surface. That's the signal that's hardest to get from automated metrics alone, and it's what 44,387 votes of this kind are particularly good at providing.
